Which Properties Are Inspected and What Do Inspectors Find

The vast majority of inspections were conducted at multi-family dwellings, including apartment buildings and residential properties ranging from small 5-unit walk-ups to large complexes with 100 or more units. Hotels account for most of the remainder, with a small number of SRO, residential hotel, and general housing inspections. This reflects the composition of LA County’s rental housing stock subject to routine Environmental Health inspection.

What are these properties failing? The most common violations involve the parts of a building that tenants share every day: hallways, stairwells, common areas, and trash areas. Peeling paint, damaged walls and ceilings, broken screens, and debris are the conditions inspectors found most often. These are not sudden failures. They are the kinds of problems that build up over time in buildings that are not being regularly maintained.

What This Suggests

92% of these inspections, 44,310 of 48,085, happened at multi-family rental buildings of five or more units. Whatever inspectors find in this dataset, renters are the ones living with it.

Three patterns in what's being found
Building fabric
Wall, ceiling, and floor damage is the largest grouping, more than 1 in 4 of all 37,026 violations; the two largest single categories are common-area damage (the most frequent violation type overall, 3,553 records) and unit-interior damage (2,254).
Shared spaces
Seven of the ten most frequent violation types are common-area conditions: hallways, stairwells, refuse areas, the parts of a building no single tenant controls.
Severity profile
94% of violations are non-critical, the maintenance-and-habitability tier rather than emergencies, a pattern of slow, ongoing wear rather than sudden failure.

This combination matters for who can act on it. A tenant can report conditions inside their own unit. The most common violations in this dataset sit in spaces no individual tenant is responsible for, in buildings where the owner receives the inspection report and the correction deadlines. When the inspector doesn't come, the conditions most likely to go unrecorded are precisely the shared-space ones.

The County's own Healthy Homes Advisory, printed on the inspection report itself, warns that water intrusion “can lead to mold growth and costly structural damage.” Fabric damage is the category that compounds: what costs a repair when found early costs a remediation when found late.

The questions this raises
When the most frequent violations are in shared spaces, who in the building reports them?
And what is accumulating, unrecorded, in the buildings that have not seen an inspector since 2023?
