Timeline

The institutional history of housing inspection oversight in LA County, from the 1960s contracts through the close of the study period.

1960s
City public health contracts executed
LA County signs public health services contracts with incorporated cities, placing housing inspections under County authority through the Department of Public Health. Many of these contracts are never updated after their original execution.
Six decades pass.
The contracts stay in force unchanged. When the California Legislature reorganizes the Health and Safety Code in 1991, the contracts are left citing superseded law. Throughout, DIB conducts routine inspections of apartment buildings with five or more units, and at no point in these sixty years does any version of the contracts require an inspection schedule for incorporated cities.
Jan 2023
Dataset begins
This analysis captures DIB routine inspection activity beginning January 1, 2023, across 85 incorporated cities and a small number of unincorporated area records. Of 48,085 unique inspections, 237 are from unincorporated areas, representing less than 1% of all records.
Apr 2024
RHHP Ordinance adopted by unanimous vote of the LA County Board of Supervisors
The Rental Housing Habitability Program was created for unincorporated LA County areas, mandating routine inspections every four years. Incorporated cities remain outside RHHP scope. DIB continues to operate in incorporated cities without a mandated inspection frequency.
Oct 2024
85-City Public Health Contracts updated, the first update in roughly 60 years
LA County DPH executed updated public health services contracts with 85 incorporated cities. The contracts modernized legal language and statutory references. No mandated inspection frequency was added for incorporated cities. Source: Board letter, October 8, 2024 (PDF)
Nov 2024
RHHP launches in unincorporated areas
The new inspection program became operational, covering unincorporated communities including East LA, Altadena, Lennox, and Florence-Firestone. Incorporated cities remain under DIB jurisdiction.
Jan 2025
SB 1465 and AB 548 take effect
SB 1465 expands public agency enforcement tools for substandard housing. AB 548 requires inspectors to examine neighboring units when substandard conditions are identified. Both laws apply to DIB inspections.
Sep 2025
Dataset ends
An 82% decline in DIB inspections is documented from the January 2023 baseline, with the violation rate rising from 32% to 42%. The cause of the decline cannot be determined from the available data alone; whether budget reductions, staffing changes, or program restructuring were responsible remains unknown.

Key Terms and Definitions

Click any term to expand its definition and official source. Terms are grouped by type.

Jurisdiction

A city that has its own elected city council and city government, formally established as a municipal corporation. LA County has 88 incorporated cities. Three, Long Beach, Pasadena, and Vernon, run their own municipal health departments and are not part of DIB's contract cities. The other 85 contract with LA County DPH for public health services, including DIB's routine housing inspections.

Source: County of Los Angeles, 88 Cities, Incorporation and Population (PDF)

A community that is not part of any incorporated city. More than 65% of LA County's land area and roughly one million residents fall within unincorporated areas, where the County itself, not a city government, provides municipal services and the Board of Supervisors functions as the local city council. RHHP's four-year mandated inspection cycle applies only to unincorporated areas. Incorporated cities, where DIB has no mandated inspection frequency, are not covered by RHHP.

Source: County of Los Angeles, Unincorporated Areas (PDF)

Agencies

The District Inspection Branch is the unit within LA County's Environmental Health Division that ensures residential housing countywide is, in the County's words, “safe, sanitary, and fit for human habitation.” According to the County, DIB inspects roughly 60,000 apartment buildings of five or more units plus about 12,000 associated swimming pools and spas, in cities that have adopted the County Health Code, and responds to complaints at smaller properties. All 48,085 inspection records in this dataset were collected by DIB.

Source: DIB Housing Inspections page

The LA County Department of Public Health is the county agency responsible for protecting and improving the health of all LA County residents. DPH oversees the Environmental Health Division, which the County describes as “responsible for the enforcement and education of Federal, State, and local laws and regulations relating to environmental factors which affect public health and safety.” The division comprises five branches, including District Inspection, plus two support units. DPH administers public health services contracts with 85 incorporated cities across LA County.

Source: Environmental Health Division

The Los Angeles Housing Department is a City of LA agency, separate from LA County DPH, that oversees housing policy, rent stabilization, and tenant protections within city limits. LAHD administers the Systematic Code Enforcement Program and enforces the Los Angeles Housing Code.

Source: LAHD, Who We Are

Programs

Created by LA County Ordinance 2024-0020, adopted unanimously by the Board of Supervisors on April 23, 2024, effective October 23, 2024, with inspections beginning November 2024. RHHP mandates routine housing inspections every four years in unincorporated LA County areas only. Incorporated cities remain under DIB jurisdiction. RHHP activity is not reflected in this dataset.

Source: RHHP program page Source: Official RHHP flyer (PDF)

The City of LA's proactive inspection program for rental housing, administered by LAHD. SCEP inspects all rental properties with two or more units in the City of LA at least once every four years, enforcing the Los Angeles Housing Code. SCEP activity is not included in this dataset, which reflects DIB inspections only.

Source: LA Housing Department, Inspections and Fees Source: Official SCEP brochure (PDF)

The Housing Inspection Guide defines the HOIR as “the notice issued by the Department to property owners.” Completed by a DIB Environmental Health Specialist, it records each violation, its severity classification, the applicable code sections, and required corrective actions, along with which units were inspected or unavailable. The RHHP program in unincorporated areas uses a similar report called the RHOIR.

Source: Housing Inspection Guide (PDF)

Inspection Types and Metrics

The Housing Inspection Guide defines a routine inspection as “a periodic, unannounced inspection to determine compliance with all applicable federal, state, and local statutes, orders, ordinances, quarantines, rules, regulations, or directives related to the public health.” All 48,085 records in this dataset are routine inspections, conducted regardless of whether anyone filed a complaint. Unlike SCEP inspections in the City of LA, which come with 30 days written notice, DIB routine inspections are unannounced.

Source: Housing Inspection Guide (PDF)

A reactive visit triggered when a tenant, neighbor, or other party reports a housing problem to DIB. The source file identifies inspection type in its service description field; every record in this extract is marked Routine, so complaint inspections are absent from this analysis despite appearing in the dataset’s name.

Source: Housing Inspection Guide (PDF)

For properties with 60 or more units, DIB inspectors are required to attempt inspection of at least 10% of units per visit. In a 100-unit building, as few as 10 units may be entered and assessed. This means violation counts in large buildings reflect sampled conditions rather than a complete survey of the property.

Source: Housing Inspection Guide (PDF)

Legal Standards

A condition that poses a direct and immediate threat to the health and safety of occupants. Examples include vermin found inside a dwelling unit, no hot water, or inoperable smoke detectors. Critical violations require prompt corrective action. They represent 6% of violations in this dataset.

Source: Housing Inspection Guide (PDF)

A condition that affects habitability and living conditions but does not pose an immediate danger to occupants. Examples include holes or water damage to walls, missing window screens, or inadequate lighting in common areas. Non-critical violations represent 94% of violations in this dataset.

Source: Housing Inspection Guide (PDF)

The section of the Los Angeles County Code that governs health and safety standards for residential housing. It defines what constitutes a violation, how violations are classified, and what corrective actions are required. DIB enforces Title 11 within incorporated cities that have adopted the County Health Code by ordinance or resolution; not all contracted cities have adopted the housing provisions, and the County’s DIB page notes that Glendale and Torrance have not.

Source: LA County Code, Title 11 (Municode)

Agencies and Responsibilities

Multiple agencies oversee housing in LA County. Each operates under different legal authority, covers different jurisdictions, and applies a different lens to the same problem.

County - Public Health Authority

LA County Dept. of Public Health

DPH · County agency

Oversees all public health services across LA County. Administers contracts with 85 incorporated cities.

Environmental Health Division

EH · Division of DPH

Enforces health and safety codes within contracted cities. Houses the District Inspection Branch.

District Inspection Branch

Data source

DIB · Branch of EH

Routine inspections of 5+ unit buildings across 85 incorporated cities. No mandated frequency. Source of all data in this project.

Rental Housing Habitability Program

New 2024

RHHP · Under DPH directly · Unincorporated areas only

Launched November 2024. Mandates inspections every four years in unincorporated LA County only. Does not cover incorporated cities.

vs

City of LA - Housing Authority

Los Angeles Housing Department

LAHD · City of LA agency

Entirely separate from LA County DPH. Oversees housing policy, rent stabilization, and tenant protections. Administers SCEP.

Systematic Code Enforcement Program

SCEP · Under LAHD · City of LA only

Inspects 2+ unit buildings in City of LA at least once every four years. Enforces the Los Angeles Housing Code. Not in this dataset.

Where they overlap: City of LA neighborhoods

DIB inspects

5 or more unit buildings · County public health law · No mandated frequency

+

SCEP inspects

2 or more unit buildings · City housing code · Every 4 years mandated

Both programs inspect properties in City of LA neighborhoods simultaneously but under different legal authority and enforced by different types of inspectors. DIB inspectors are Environmental Health Specialists enforcing county public health law. SCEP inspectors are city housing code officers enforcing the Los Angeles Municipal Code. They are not substitutes for each other. When DIB inspections decline, tenants lose county-level public health oversight even where city housing inspections continue.
